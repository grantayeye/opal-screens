Inside NanoPix: What 0.7mm Pixel Pitch Actually Delivers

There's a moment that happens with every Onyx Series install. The client walks in for the first time, crosses the room, and — instead of stopping at their seat — keeps walking toward the wall. They get close. Closer than they'd ever watch from. And then they stop, stare for a moment, and say some version of the same thing: "I can't find the pixels."

That's not a coincidence. It's the whole point of the Onyx Series, and it's the result of two technologies working together in a way that, honestly, pushes what we thought was possible in MicroLED displays.

This article breaks down what 0.7mm pixel pitch actually means in practice, what NanoPix adds on top of that, and which spaces truly benefit from having the finest pitch available.

What 0.7mm Actually Means

Most conversations about pixel pitch start and end with the viewing distance formula: multiply the pixel pitch by 1,000 to get the minimum comfortable viewing distance in millimeters. At 0.7mm, that works out to roughly 0.7 meters — a little over two feet. Sit any closer than two feet on a conventional 0.9mm or 1.2mm panel, and you'd start to resolve individual pixels. On the Onyx, you don't.

But the number alone doesn't tell the whole story. The reason 0.7mm is meaningfully different from 0.9mm — not just mathematically, but visually — comes down to pixel density. The Onyx carries approximately 204 pixels per inch, which is solidly in tablet-display territory. When you're sitting six feet away watching a film, the display is rendering at a resolution your eye simply cannot pull apart. The image stops being a collection of lit points and starts behaving like a window.

That shift — from "display" to "window" — is what clients are reacting to when they walk up close and can't find a seam.

The NanoPix Calibration Layer

Getting to 0.7mm pixel pitch is one engineering challenge. Getting every pixel at 0.7mm to behave identically is a different one entirely, and it's the one that NanoPix is built to solve.

MicroLED panels are assembled from individual modules, and individual modules are made from individual LEDs — thousands of them, each manufactured with slight variations in output, color, and forward voltage. On most displays, those variations add up to something you'd never notice from 10 feet but would catch immediately from 3 feet: slight brightness drift across the panel, color temperature that shifts from one corner to another, faint banding where one module meets the next.

NanoPix addresses this at the pixel level. Every pixel on every module is individually measured, and the calibration values are burned into the display controller. The result is a panel where brightness uniformity holds to within a fraction of a percent across the entire surface and color accuracy stays locked across the full range of content — whites are white, not slightly warm in the upper left and slightly cool in the lower right.

This is the kind of detail that matters enormously in close-viewing applications and barely at all in a large conference room where the screen is 25 feet away. NanoPix exists specifically because the Onyx is designed to be viewed up close, and up close, uniformity is everything.

BlackFire on the Onyx

The Onyx Series also carries BlackFire, the anti-glare matte nano-coating applied at the module level across the full Opal lineup (with the exception of the Doublet). In a close-viewing environment, glare management becomes even more important than at conventional viewing distances. The angles are different, the room lighting hits the panel at different positions as you move around, and any reflective surface will catch your eye before the content does.

BlackFire eliminates that without the contrast penalty that traditional anti-glare treatments introduce. The coating sits between the LED substrate and the viewing surface, scattering ambient light before it reaches the emitting layer. Contrast stays intact — the 10,000:1 spec holds in real-world room conditions — while the surface reads as matte and neutral regardless of where the room lighting is positioned.

For a dedicated private theater or art-display installation, that combination of true black and glare-free surface is what lets the content fully command the room.

Where 0.7mm Makes the Most Sense

It's worth being direct here: the Onyx is not the right call for every project. The Boulder Series at 0.9mm with SilkStream 240Hz is the recommendation for most high-end home theaters — the combination of motion performance, HDR headroom at 1,000 nits, and that pixel pitch covers the vast majority of residential installs extremely well. The Crystal at 0.9mm handles living rooms and media rooms. There's a reason those are the volume products.

But there are specific applications where 0.7mm and NanoPix are not just better but genuinely the only appropriate answer.

Private screening rooms with close seating configurations. If the design calls for a single row of recliners at 8 feet or a loveseat at 5 feet, the Onyx is the right call. The viewing geometry is intimate enough that the extra pixel density is immediately visible, and the NanoPix uniformity means the image holds up under that level of scrutiny.

Digital art and gallery installations. High-resolution art content — whether that's a rotating museum-quality collection or a single commissioned piece — is designed to be studied, not just watched. Viewers walk up to it. They look at brushstroke-level detail. The Onyx handles that use case in a way that coarser pitches simply don't.

Study and reading room displays. A home office or private library where a large display is used for reading, document work, and reference material benefits enormously from that pixel density. Text rendering at 0.7mm is a different experience from text rendering at 1.2mm, and anyone who spends time working at their display will feel the difference immediately.

Compact installations in premium spaces. When the room constraints push the display size down and the seating distance inward — a bedroom, a yacht cabin, a private jet interior — the Onyx delivers an image that punches well above what the physical scale would suggest is possible.

What Clients Report After Installation

The consistent theme in post-install conversations is that the Onyx removes something people didn't realize was bothering them. They don't describe it as "sharper" or "higher resolution" — they describe it as the display disappearing. The screen stops calling attention to itself as a screen. Content just exists in the room.

That's a subjective description of a technical reality: when pixel density exceeds the resolving capability of the human eye at a given distance, the perceptual system stops processing it as a grid and starts processing it as a continuous surface. At 0.7mm from typical close-viewing distances, that threshold is comfortably crossed.

The NanoPix uniformity contributes to this in a way that's harder to articulate but equally real. A panel with brightness drift draws the eye toward the brighter region, even if you don't consciously notice it. A perfectly calibrated panel doesn't compete with the content for your attention. It just presents the image and gets out of the way.

The Honest Comparison

If you're building a dedicated home theater and your integrator is walking you through the product options, here's the honest framing: the Boulder Series at 0.9mm with SilkStream will cover nearly everything you want from a cinematic viewing experience, and it does it at a price point that leaves room for the rest of the room design. For most projects, that's the conversation.

The Onyx becomes the conversation when close-viewing clarity is non-negotiable — when the application demands that someone standing three feet from the panel sees nothing but image. When you're building something intended to look indistinguishable from reality. When the display itself needs to be invisible.

That's what 0.7mm and NanoPix actually deliver. Not a spec sheet number, but the experience of a display that stops being a display.
